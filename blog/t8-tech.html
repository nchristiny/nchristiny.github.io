Regular Expressions

or: All my Regex-es live in Texas

August 9 2015

Regex marks the spot

I found out recently that I am in the learning stage termed as: "early programmer" - not quite a novice still, but not quite experienced enough to have accomplished my own projects or meaningful contributions to other open-source projects. (Soon!)

I have grasped so far that learning to program is like deciphering a puzzle in a foreign language.

This week we were asked to pick a topic and write about, so I chose Regular Expressions (regex) since it was something with which I am not yet familiar, and had heard spoken about in hushed tones among the cognoscenti. So here goes:

To efficiently find a given string, any pattern of characters, you can use regex. This can include finding a file name or some piece of text within a large file or database. As you can see, this can save a lot of time and energy, so naturally programmers love them.

There are tons of different meta characters and quantifiers to refine your matches, so lets run through a couple of examples. Please bear with me as I am still learning as an absolute beginner with regex.

From what I can best understand, regex is largely language independent in basic function, something that I found remarkable, even so there are naturally differences between Perl, PHP, Python, Ruby (and so on) regex.

The search expression, (hereafter referred to as string), that you are locating can be substituted with any number of fancy escape characters, and variables. Regex will scan from left to right and stop at the first match, but it is possible to go on and "find next" depending on the programming language you are using. Say you only want a word to match halfway and then you want the next 5 characters as well. You can do that with quantifiers.

As I mentioned, quantifier characters such as * and ? determine how many characters before or after a certain target to search.

To go into a couple of examples, say you wish to search for the incidences of a word and a number, like a street address. You can use [0-9] to specify all digits and then the literal word characters.

\d specifies a digit, just as [0-9]. These are called shorthand character classes. \s stands for whitespace. There are quite a few more like this, and I actually got lost reading about a lot of them. The resource I found was here: Regular-Expressions.info .

There is so much material on regex that I feel like I could get lost forever, so I wish to just summarize and say that regex allows us to rapidly find what we are looking for, provided we use a little variable and logic magic.

Thanks for reading!
