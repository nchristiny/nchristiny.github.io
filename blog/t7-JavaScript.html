JavaScript Vs. Ruby

WHO WILL WIN?

August 2 2015

It's not a competition!

JavaScript was terra incognita for me as I started this week. I had a little knowledge of it from a few self paced intro courses from much earlier, and back then I remember feeling it was quite different from your run-of-the-mill HTML/CSS, (you can make web pages interactive with it! wow!), but difficult to understand.

Having gone through these weeks with Ruby, what was once unfamiliar has been made clearer; although the syntax has changed, the concepts are the much the same. I can really see why Ruby is such a beloved language and also to a certain extent some of the beauty and power of JavaScript.

Ruby is well liked as it is very literal and a semantic language - it can be read like we humans talk. JavaScript not so much, but there are so many similarities that I felt the crossover was much the same as when you are learning two foreign languages at the same time. The risk there from time to time is that you might mix up the grammar or some other rules or spelling.

To name a specific example of how JavaScript and Ruby are similar yet weirdly different, I can just point to declaring a simple loop, a basic feature of any program.

For Ruby, it is not a thing, as they say, to make a loop:

i=0
while i <= 5
  puts "I see #{i}"
  i +=1
end

=begin Output: 
I see 0
I see 1
I see 2
I see 3
I see 4
I see 5
=end

Basic Ruby loop, pretty as you please, as you can see.

On the other hand, JavaScript, (and I think this sentiment is shared by most of the folks in our cohort to whom coding and specifically JavaScript is new), lacks a certain finesse, shall we say. However, I find that there is hidden simplicity all its own once you start understanding it. I am far from this point right now, but hope to get close to it soon, seeing as JavaScript is widely used in todays Web. 

for (var i = 0; i <= 5; ++i){
  console.log("I see " + i);
};

/* Output: 
I see 0
I see 1
I see 2
I see 3
I see 4
I see 5
*/

Yes, there is a certain beauty to JavaScript, and it is an extremely forgiving language- meaning it can pop an automatic type conversion or coercion on you when things aren't quite syntactically up to snuff. I feel that this, coupled with the fact that the error messages are often cryptic is why most new programmers such as myself feel that Ruby is a somehow a "better" language. Naturally this is not true; they are just different. As the saying goes, (big fan of clich√©s,) the right tool for the right job; and the more tools in your toolbox, then the better equipped you'll be to face challenges.

In summary this week was spent getting my head around JavaScript and trying to keep the previous Ruby learning present in my neural connections at the same time. Both languages have their strong and weak points. Thanks for reading!
