Phase 0 Resources

MASTERLIST

August 16 2015

An early coder reference.

Here is a huge list of helpful links, cheat-sheets and other resources that aided me during Phase 0 for Dev Bootcamp. I have compiled them here for my future reference and also to help anyone else who may come across it. Enjoy responsibly.

Study aides, tools and tweaks

  • 9 Ambient Sound Apps to Increase Your Study Focus.
  • Some other music/soundscape apps I have used are: Songza, and Noisli.
  • Focus Booster is a free desktop app for OS X or Windows, and is based on the Pomodoro Technique. Tweak it as you see fit. For example, I like my focus sessions to last a bit longer, so I adjusted the timer's settings. I have not used the online paid service, I just use the free desktop timer to time-box.
  • Tuts+ code offers articles, tutorials and a nice newsletter with free and discount offers.
  • For example, Tuts+ offers this free Sublime Text tutorial: Perfect Workflow in Sublime Text 2 - (still largely relevant for Sublime Text 3.)
  • Speaking of Sublime Text, I recommend this blog post on the GA blog. Pay special regard to the package control section to unlock Sublime Text's full potential. In my opinion, this is the main reason to use Sublime Text, (apart from the aesthetics!)
  • iTerm 2, a Mac OS X terminal emulator that does amazing things, such as split panes, autocomplete, paste history, instant replays. Free with suggested donation.
  • This blog post by John Resig talks about the importance of practicing your craft everyday - without fail. Related to Jerry Seinfeld's lifehack: 'Don't break the chain'. Printable calendars (A4 paper size) to use with this technique are available here: here.
  • HackDesign offers a free course on UX, UI, graphic design principles, typography and best practices. They also offer a page called Toolkit, a large list of popular tools used by professionals in the industry. Free, sign up for the newsletter for goodies.
  • Speaking of free goodies, I always learn something new or receive a free offers (such as free vector icons etc) from Webdesigner News, "Curated stories for designers. Their newsletter is regularly jam-packed with great articles.
  • No tutorial list would be complete without a reference to Tutorials Point, an exhaustive resource to many, many programming languages.
  • The free eBook "Becoming a Better Programmer" from Stephen Young's website, Better Programmer. There are a number of cool, informative articles here and the book itself is very comprehensive. Highly recommended.
  • Also I should mention this free eBook by Mark Boulton, Designing for the Web.
  • Finally I would be remiss if I did not mention Code Newbie: They are amazing. They host a Slack channel to which you may get access after the free sign up, and they host special coding hours, for example Ruby Monday, (missed a golden opportunity to host it on Tuesday!) Their Twitter Q & A's are legendary and they often have awesome podcast interviews with movers and shakers of the coding industry. Check them out at: Code Newbie and follow on Twitter: @CodeNewbies. They regularly send out free stuff as well and discount codes for online classes. Highly recommended for the early and beginner coder.

bash & Command Line

  • Obviously, Zed Shaw's Command Line Crash Course is a good place to start to get your bash on and to get a quick introduction to Unix operating system.
  • Speaking of Unix, once you are ready to take a much more in-depth plunge, check out the extensive online book, The Art of Unix Programming by Eric Steven Raymond. If that seems too daunting at first, at least read the first chapter summary: Basics of the Unix Philosophy. These rules, such as Least Surprise, Modularity, Parsimony are principles that are shared across many different programming languages, so it really helps to understand these concepts early.
  • To practice your shortcut skills, try Shortcut Foo and typing lessons. The latter link employs a good range of different coding languages for a nice challenge. These sites have paid options, but the basic lessons are free.
  • You can go right to the Bash Reference Manual for the nitty gritty details.

Git and GitHub

  • Roger Dudler's handy git cheat sheet: git - the simple guide is "just a simple guide for getting started with git. no deep shit ;)"
  • As a corollary to the above, Sam Livingston-Gray's Think Like (a) Git is an invaluable resource.
  • GitHub provides a handy dandy Styleguide for HTML & CSS, JavaScript, and Ruby. "It's dangerous to go alone. Take this!"
  • Dev Bootcamp recommended ebook: Tower's Learn Version Control with Git.

HTML/CSS & design principles

  • I made use of the Head First series of books. For a comprehensive, (if somewhat overwhelming) survey of HTML books to consider, see 100 Best HTML & HTML5 Books to Learn HTML From by Brad Zomick on skilledup.
  • Of course, the Codecademy HTML/CSS track is great.
  • Wireframe online services: Writemaps and for something much more stripped down, try Tall Tree's Wireframe tool. My cohort fellows liked Balsamiq as well.
  • For designing moodboards, visit Gomoodboard.
  • After mastering the basics, I found it useful to read up on latest developments in the industry and new frameworks, for example Sass & Compass.
  • Of course this also included a survey of Bootstrap.
  • An exhaustive list of Bootstrap resources is located here: The Big Badass List of Twitter Bootstrap Resources.
  • For a programmer in a designer's world perspective of Bootstrap, check out this post by Anna Powell-Smith.
  • While on the subject of frameworks, check out Foundation by Zurb and the associated Zurb University resources.
  • HTML5 Rocks!
  • CSS Tricks!
  • When it is time to run with the big dogs, level up to Mozilla Developer site and the related blog site: Mozilla Hacks.
  • Quirksmode is an invaluable cross-browser and mobile compatibility tester. Check out creator Peter-Paul Koch's informative blog posts too.
  • Responsive Design and Mobile First? See these GIFs, read the original article by Ethan Marcotte, and pick up the book.
  • Of course, we cannot speak of Responsive Design without also mentioning Responsible Responsive Design by Scott Jehl.
  • While you're at it, read this seminal article by John Allsopp, A Dao of Web Design. I recommend subscribing to the A List Apart email alerts.
  • For even yet more on responsive design, I found that this post by Saran Chamling was useful: 40 Responsive Navigation Solutions (Examples & Codes). You can find a nice variety of techniques here using jQuery, plain old CSS and JavaScript.
  • HTML Arrows by Zeppelin provides a great place to look for HTML symbol codes in a convenient repository. For even more, see the Unicode Character Code Charts.
  • Favicons should be easy to implement. Real Favicon Generator helps get them right, cross platform and cross browser.
  • No list on HTML5 resources should miss this: HTML5 Doctor. For example, here is a nice little flowchart for sectioning HTML5 elements: HTML5 Element Flowchart.
  • HTML5 Outliner is an online outliner. Pretty neat.
  • Once you get your code, you need to validate it. Use these online tools to see if your pages pass muster. Nu HTML Checker, and the official W3C Markup Validation Service.
  • For publishing HTML friendly code-snippets, try Hilite. You can choose the style, (ah there you are Monokai, my old friend.)
  • HTML5 Boilerplate and Initializr are two supremely useful tools that should be part of your arsenal as a developer. You should at least know the existence of these tools.
  • Ever wonder what the purpose of the robots.txt file is? Wonder no more: About /robots.txt. Check out the corollary: About /humans.txt.
  • CSS Stats will give you a shakedown visualization on stats concerning the CSS of any webpage. Try it out on your favorite sites or your own.
  • Great blog post by Andrew Houle, CSS Tips I Wish I Knew When I First Started. Keep in mind this blog post was from quite a while ago and the state of CSS is in constant flux, as most things are in this industry, so be sure and find out the latest techniques yourself to stay current. See above links for CSS Tricks et al.
  • Clear a float in CSS from SitePoint blog post by Alex Walker. Again, note that it is quite old and you should always be prepared with the salt shaker.
  • Eric Meyer's website, the creator of reset.css and many other nice things. His site offers blog posts, tools, plugins, and other useful snippets and hacks.
  • A kind of CSS validation tool, CSS Lint has the capacity to ferret out the most obscure of problems or potential pitfalls in your stylesheets. "It does basic syntax checking as well as applying a set of rules to the code that look for problematic patterns or signs of inefficiency."
  • When you absolutely posiively have to find the specificty of something, use the online CSS Specificity Calculator.
  • CSS Gradients are still a pain to write, but at least there is a tool that helps: Ultimate CSS Gradient Generator from ColorZilla. I use the ColorZilla Chrome plug in and really like it. To find a completely randomized CSS Color Gradient, roll the dice on this randomized gradient generator. On a related note, Paletton Live Colorizer will help you find the right colors you need. "It is designed to create palettes of colors that work well together according to classical color theories."
  • If you ever need to add more color names to your vocabulary, check out Crayon.css. "Crayon.css is a list of CSS variables linking color names to hexadecimal values, usable with postCSS or with your favorite CSS preprocessor, being it Stylus, Sass or Less."
  • A to Z CSS videos are a series of great bite-sized tutorials on a particular topic in CSS by Guy Routledge.
  • I could go on and on with this section on CSS and HTML, but I think I will wrap it up with some links to typographic resources: 40 Latest High-Quality Free Fonts For Professional Designers, the online calculator for whitespace, Contrast Ratio, font size calculator: Modular Scale calculator and the related site, Type-Scale.
  • Check out this article by Miguel Palhas on 3D CSS design: Perfecting a CSS 3D animation.
  • I highly recommend getting a free CodePen account and start experimenting yourself. Follow them @codepen for absolutely stunning work by the community. Some of the work is truly awe inspiring.
  • Finally, from a purely design perspective, beware of anyone who claims that your webpage should "obey" the Golden Ratio or Golden Rectangle or any other sort of mystical nonsense. Watch this short video to learn how this notion has been thoroughly debunked: Debunking 10 Golden Ratio Myths.

  • Poignant Guide to Ruby by _why the Lucky Stiff.

JavaScript

  • Seven JavaScript Things I Wish I Knew Much Earlier In My Career, article by Christian Heilmann in Smashing Magazine, an excellent resource in of itself.
  • CodeCombat, a fun and easy way to learn code (not just JavaScript, but that's what I have used.) Totally addictive.

  • Fun little interactive learning tool, Learning Advanced JavaScript by John Resig.
  • Finally I found this article informative: Immediately-Invoked Function Expression (IIFE) by Ben Alman.

    • General Recommendations

  • Make a professional Twitter account. Follow people in the industry.
  • Get a Rubber Duckie. Or use a significant other if they can stand it. I sometimes employ my cats as stand-in rubber duckies.
  • Watch all the Dev Talks videos that are provided.
  • Try all of the optional exercises that are suggested.
  • Do the assigned reading, (which sounds obvious, but you would be surprised...)
  • Quit your day job. (Obviously for most folks this may not be an option before On-Site Phase 1, but if you can swing it, I say go for it.)
  • Stay positive. I know this sounds really flippant and easy for someone to just say, but sometimes you may feel like you have made the wrong decision. If you feel you are in the weeds, do not dwell on it. Take a walk, a shower, drink a cup of tea or a beer, take a break, and try again. And above all else, ASK FOR HELP. Your cohort, your guides and tutors, even random Twitter citizens can lend a helping elbow to help prop you back up and send you on your way. Believe in yourself. Don't give up.
